**2. What explains the erosion of the cash balance?**
### Slide 1

Let's look at where our cash is physically sitting. Bob and Maggie made a strategic shift to sell more mature, 'instant landscape' plants, which can be sold for a premium price. However, much of this inventory takes two to five years to grow sufficiently for it to be sold. They also increased the plant varieties by more than 40%. On top of that, to win business from small nurseries, they offered slight improvements in credit terms. The left chart shows the result: their cash keeps on dropping year by year, with an increase of unsold inventory because of many unpaid customer invoices, showing as the increase of the Accounts Receivable.
### Slide 2

Now, let's look at the other side—why cash is leaving. Bob expects revenue to hit a record 30% growth rate in 2016. But if you look at the chart, our Accounts Payable is practically zero. Because Maggie strictly pays suppliers early enough to obtain trade discounts, like the one mentioned being the 2% discount for paying within 10 days—cash leaves from their account immediately. Combining this with their strict aversion to debt financing to avoid inventory risk, this means that they are funding their rapid growth using their own money, keeping them from accumalating their available liquidity.
### Slide 3

To really understand the severity, we have to look at the industry benchmarks. The industry standard for holding inventory is about 386.3 days, but we are holding ours for 476.3 days. Our competitors collect cash in about 21.8 days, but we are letting customers stretch it to 50.9 days. And worst of all, while the industry holds cash for 26.9 days before paying suppliers, we empty our accounts in 9.9 days. Compared to the industry, they arent doing so well, and with the unbalance of Accounts receivable and Accounts payable, they are paying suppliers too early, while getting their money too late from customers, meaning that the liquidity at some point has to break.
### Slide 4

As a long-term solution, we shouldn't completely drop the mature plants because they deliver our best profit margins. However, we can't afford to keep burying all our cash in the dirt for years at a time. We need to rebalance our pipeline. By shifting some future plantings toward 'quick-flip' annuals, we can generate faster, more reliable cash flow to fund the growth of those high-margin mature plants
